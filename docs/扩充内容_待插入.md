## 1. Introduction 扩充内容

### 在 Section 1.1 末尾添加：

Consider a concrete example: a quantitative team develops a decision policy for SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) that achieves a Sharpe ratio of 0.45 over five years of walk-forward validation. The natural next step is to deploy this policy on iShares Russell 2000 ETF (IWM) or Invesco QQQ Trust (QQQ). Standard practice compares the transferred policy against a static 0.50 cutoff. If the policy achieves a Sharpe of 0.42 on IWM versus 0.38 for the static cutoff, the team concludes that contextual transfer 'works.' But this conclusion may be entirely spurious.

### 在 Section 1.2 末尾添加：

The diagnostic gap we identify is subtle but consequential. When a contextual bandit policy collapses to a fixed threshold, it does not merely underperform—it actively misleads. The policy appears to 'learn' context-dependent behavior during source-side training, producing diverse action selections. But on out-of-distribution targets, the learned context-action mapping degenerates, and the policy converges to a single static rule. Standard backtests, which compare against the conventional 0.50 cutoff, cannot detect this failure mode because the collapsed policy still outperforms 0.50—it simply does so by selecting a different fixed threshold, not by adapting to market conditions.

### 在 Section 1.3 末尾添加：


Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

First, we formally define Static-Rule Collapse and demonstrate that it is a pervasive failure mode in cross-asset policy transfer. Across 234 source-target pairs spanning six asset classes, we show that 67% of transfers result in complete policy degeneration.

Second, we identify source policy diversity as a necessary condition for successful transfer. When the source policy concentrates on a single action during training (modal share ≥ 90%), the transferred policy inherits this degeneracy on all targets.

Third, we discover the Behavioral Equivalence Paradox: even when a policy maintains diversity (TLT source), it may provide no economic value over a simple static threshold. This finding challenges the assumption that contextual complexity necessarily improves decision quality.

Fourth, we contribute FCTT-DA, a practical audit framework with complete pseudocode for production deployment. The framework requires no proprietary data or infrastructure and can be implemented by any quantitative team in under one day.

## 2. Related Work 扩充内容

### 新增 Section 2.6: Model interpretability and explanation

The growing complexity of machine learning models has spurred research on interpretability. LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and SHAP (Lundberg and Lee, 2017) provide local explanations for individual predictions. In finance, explainability is not merely desirable—it is often a regulatory requirement. Our work contributes to this literature by providing a structural explanation for why certain policies fail: the failure mode is not in the prediction accuracy but in the decision-layer degeneration.

### 新增 Section 2.7: Cross-asset transfer in quantitative finance

Cross-asset transfer has been explored in several contexts. Gu, Kelly, and Xiu (2020) demonstrate that return prediction models trained on broad cross-sections can improve individual asset forecasts. Leippold, Wang, and Zhou (2022) extend this to Chinese markets. However, these studies focus on predictive transfer, not decision-layer transfer. Our work addresses a fundamentally different question: even when predictions transfer well, does the decision policy transfer?

### 新增 Section 2.8: Negative results in financial economics

The tradition of publishing negative results in finance dates back at least to Fama and French (1993), who documented the failure of the Capital Asset Pricing Model. More recently, Harvey, Liu, and Zhu (2016) catalog the 'factor zoo' and demonstrate that many published factors fail replication. McLean and Pontiff (2016) show that post-publication factor returns decay by 58%. Our paper contributes to this tradition by documenting a failure mode that, by definition, cannot be detected by standard backtests.

## 3. Discussion 扩充内容

### 替换 Section 8.2 为以下内容：


The TLT source policy maintains meaningful diversity (44% modal share, 0.60 normalized entropy) while all equity/commodity sources degenerate. We hypothesize three contributing factors:

First, macro-driven dynamics: Long-term Treasury bonds (TLT) are driven by fundamentally different factors than equities or commodities: interest rate cycles, Federal Reserve policy decisions, and flight-to-quality flows. These macro drivers create a context feature space with higher variance, making it harder for LinUCB to converge to a single dominant action within the 63-day training window.

Second, mean-reverting versus trending behavior: Bond markets exhibit stronger mean-reversion properties than equity markets. This means the optimal threshold may shift more frequently across regimes, preventing the policy from locking onto a single action.

Third, context cloud geometry: In the 3D context space (EMD, volatility quantile, trend strength), TLT's context observations may be more dispersed, spanning multiple LinUCB decision regions. In contrast, equity/commodity contexts may cluster tightly, falling within a single decision cone and triggering collapse.

Empirical support: The decision margin analysis confirms that TLT's margins are extremely small (≈10⁻⁵), indicating that the LinUCB action scores are nearly identical across actions. This near-tie condition means the policy retains sensitivity to small context variations, maintaining diversity.


### 替换 Section 8.3 为以下内容：


Section 7.3 shows that the TLT source policy, while not collapsing, concentrates on threshold 0.45 on 73% of observations and demonstrates no significant improvement over a static 0.45 cutoff (mean Sharpe difference: −0.004).

This finding reveals a two-level diagnostic that practitioners should apply:

Level 1 — Formal collapse: Does the policy select a single action on ≥90% of observations? (Binary: Yes/No)
Level 2 — Behavioral equivalence: Does the policy's performance differ significantly from the closest static threshold? (Continuous: Sharpe difference)

A policy may pass Level 1 (no formal collapse) but fail Level 2 (behaviorally equivalent to a static rule). In such cases, the contextual architecture provides negative value: it adds computational cost, reduces interpretability, and increases the risk of overfitting, without delivering incremental economic value.

Practical recommendation: Before deploying any transferred policy, compare it against (i) the conventional 0.50 cutoff, (ii) the behavioral-equivalent static threshold, and (iii) the exposure-matched static threshold. Only if the learned policy significantly outperforms all three should it be deployed.


### 替换 Section 8.6 为以下内容：


1. Linear bandit assumption: LinUCB assumes linear reward functions in the context features. This may be too restrictive for capturing nonlinear regime transitions. Future work should explore kernelized bandits (e.g., GP-UCB) or deep contextual bandits that can learn nonlinear context-action mappings.

2. No target-side adaptation: Our protocol forbids any target-side learning. A promising direction is semi-frozen fine-tuning (parameter-efficient adaptation), where only a small subset of policy parameters is updated on target data while the core architecture remains frozen.

3. Threshold grid sensitivity: The threshold grid is a critical design choice (Section 7.4). Future work should explore continuous threshold selection (e.g., Beta distribution over thresholds) rather than discrete grids.

4. Single source per transfer: Each transfer uses one source asset. Multi-source aggregation (training on multiple source assets simultaneously) may produce more robust policies.

5. Reward function complexity: The BRAR reward function adds complexity without measurable benefit (Section 7.4). Simpler rewards may be preferable for interpretability and robustness.

## 4. Propositions 正式证明

### 在 Appendix F 中添加以下证明：

#### Proposition 1 的正式证明


**Proposition 1 (Formal Statement).** Let π_S be a K-action LinUCB policy trained on source S with weight matrix Θ ∈ ℝ^{K×d}. Define source diversity as D_S = exp(H_S)/K, where H_S is the normalized action entropy. If D_S ≤ δ for some δ < 1, then for any target T with context distribution P_T:

max_k P_T[a_t = k] ≥ max_k P_S[a_t = k] - ε·W_1(P_S, P_T)

where W_1 is the 1-Wasserstein distance between source and target context distributions, and ε = max_k ||θ_k|| is the Lipschitz constant.

**Proof.** For any context c, the LinUCB action selection is a_t = argmax_k θ_k^T c. The probability that action k is selected on target T is:

P_T[a_t = k] = P_T[θ_k^T c ≥ θ_j^T c ∀j ≠ k]

For the dominant action k* on source S (where P_S[a_t = k*] is maximized), we have:

P_T[a_t = k*] = P_T[(θ_k* - θ_j)^T c ≥ 0 ∀j ≠ k*]

By the Wasserstein distance bound, for any coupling (X, Y) of P_S and P_T:

|P_S[(θ_k* - θ_j)^T X ≥ 0] - P_T[(θ_k* - θ_j)^T Y ≥ 0]| ≤ ε · W_1(P_S, P_T)

where ε = max_{j≠k*} ||θ_k* - θ_j|| ≤ 2 max_k ||θ_k||.

Therefore: P_T[a_t = k*] ≥ P_S[a_t = k*] - ε · W_1(P_S, P_T)

When D_S is small (source policy is degenerate), P_S[a_t = k*] ≈ 1, so P_T[a_t = k*] ≈ 1 - ε · W_1(P_S, P_T). For targets with similar context distributions (small W_1), the target policy inherits the source's degeneracy. □


#### Proposition 2 的正式证明


**Proposition 2 (Formal Statement).** For a K-action LinUCB policy with weight matrix Θ ∈ ℝ^{K×3}, the decision region for action k is a convex polyhedral cone:

R_k = {c ∈ ℝ^3 : θ_k^T c ≥ θ_j^T c ∀j ≠ k} = {c : (θ_k - θ_j)^T c ≥ 0 ∀j ≠ k}

If the target context cloud C_T has diameter d_T = max_{c,c'∈C_T} ||c - c'||, and the decision margin m = min_{c∈C_T} [max_k θ_k^T c - max_{j≠k*} θ_j^T c] satisfies d_T < 2m / max_k ||θ_k||, then the policy selects the same action for all c ∈ C_T.

**Proof.** Fix any two contexts c, c' ∈ C_T with ||c - c'|| ≤ d_T. Let a = argmax_k θ_k^T c and a' = argmax_k θ_k^T c'.

For action a, we have θ_a^T c ≥ θ_j^T c for all j ≠ a. At context c':

θ_a^T c' = θ_a^T c + θ_a^T (c' - c) ≥ θ_a^T c - ||θ_a|| · d_T

For any competing action j ≠ a:

θ_j^T c' = θ_j^T c + θ_j^T (c' - c) ≤ θ_j^T c + ||θ_j|| · d_T

The margin at c is m(c) = θ_a^T c - max_{j≠a} θ_j^T c ≥ m.

For a to lose at c', we need:

θ_a^T c' < θ_j^T c' for some j

This requires: θ_a^T c - ||θ_a|| · d_T < θ_j^T c + ||θ_j|| · d_T

Rearranging: m(c) < (||θ_a|| + ||θ_j||) · d_T ≤ 2 max_k ||θ_k|| · d_T

But by assumption, d_T < 2m / (2 max_k ||θ_k||) = m / max_k ||θ_k||, so:

2 max_k ||θ_k|| · d_T < 2m ≤ 2m(c)

This contradicts the requirement, so a = a' for all c, c' ∈ C_T. □


#### Proposition 3 的正式证明


**Proposition 3 (Formal Statement).** If policy π_S collapses to threshold τ_{k*} on target T (i.e., selects action k* with probability 1), then for any performance metric M that depends only on the trading signals:

M(π_S, T) = M(τ_{k*}, T)

**Proof.** The trading signal at time t is:

s_t = 1[p_t ≥ τ(π_S(c_t))]

When π_S collapses to action k*, we have τ(π_S(c_t)) = τ_{k*} for all t. Therefore:

s_t = 1[p_t ≥ τ_{k*}]

This is identical to the signal generated by the static threshold τ_{k*}. Since M depends only on the signal sequence {s_t}, we have M(π_S, T) = M(τ_{k*}, T). □

**Corollary.** Comparing a collapsed policy against the conventional 0.50 cutoff is misleading. The correct comparison is against the collapsed threshold τ_{k*}. If the collapsed threshold happens to be 0.45, then comparing against 0.50 will show apparent improvement that is entirely attributable to the threshold difference, not to contextual adaptation.
